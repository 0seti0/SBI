01

Introduction

December 2013

A small, lesser-known company published a preprint showing that a machine could learn to play Atari games, including Pong, directly from high-dimensional sensory inputs. It used only raw perceptual data analogous to a visual field (pixels) and a basic motivational signal (score), with no x-y coordinates, object velocities, or other hand-engineered features.

This breakthrough initiated an algorithmic cascade in artificial intelligence, with reinforcement learning becoming a cornerstone for further innovations. DeepMind, now a subsidiary of Google, has since become renowned for state-of-the-art projects like AlphaFold and Gemini.

December 2021

The game of Pong was re-played. Not in code, but in a dish of approximately 800,000 living neurons. This experiment, termed DishBrain, marked the first demonstration of living neurons embodied in a simulated game world where they could sense information, act upon it, and learn from the consequences.

This achievement led the authors to coin the term "Synthetic Biological Intelligence (SBI)". From this point, the roots of controversy began to take shape and branch in multiple directions.

02

What is DishBrain?

The DishBrain experiment was led by a biotech startup called Cortical Labs with an aim to answer the question: "Can a disorganized cluster of living neurons, cultured in a petri dish and disconnected from a body, be compelled to exhibit intelligent, goal-directed behavior?" The primary objective was to move beyond simply modeling brain function on computer and instead, directly harness its computational power.

The brain of DishBrain is a living biological neural network (BNN) consisting of approximately 800,000 neurons sourced from either primary mouse cortical cells or human induced pluripotent stem cells. This dual sourcing enabled comparative analysis, which revealed that human-derived neurons have superior information processing capacity and performance.

The BNN is cultured on top of a high-density multi-electrode array (HD-MEA). This dense grid features approximately 26,400 tiny electrodes and is capable of recording neuronal activity from up to 1,024 channels at a sampling rate of 20 kHz. This architecture enables a dynamic, bidirectional bio-digital interface.

As the neurons grow, they naturally form synaptic connections and exhibit spontaneous electrophysiological activity. The HD-MEA can write information to the network by delivering patterned electrical stimulation to specific electrodes. Simultaneously, it can read the BNN's collective output by recording the resulting electrophysiological activity (action potentials or spikes) across the array.

The system's software interprets this neural output as a motor command, which then affects the state of Pong. The new state is then fed back to the BNN as a new pattern of sensory stimulation. This causal link between output and input establishes a functional closed loop which is essential for learning. From another perspective, neurons are connected to a world where their activity has consequences. This gives them a body and a purpose—a setup that serves as a direct, minimalist test of embodied cognition.2

Embodied cognition is a theoretical framework holding that cognition is fundamentally shaped by the body's interactions with its environment. DishBrain's embodied neurons successfully improved their performance within just five minutes of gameplay, evidenced by longer rallies and more consistent hits.

Why Did It Take Decades to Materialise?

The answer can be attributed to the timely convergence of three critical independent streams of progress:

2000s–2010s

Technological Advance: Microelectrode Arrays

The development of MEAs represents one of the significant technological revolutions in neuroscience, made possible through five decades of incremental innovation in materials science, microfabrication, electronics integration, and bioengineering.

Click to expand

Initially, MEAs were more like eavesdroppers on neural activity. They could detect when neurons fired but lacked the ability to send signals back. Their electrodes were just inert metal contacts patterned onto glass or silicon substrate.

This architecture had serious limitations. Most importantly, it was hard to scale up due to reliance on physical wires and bulky external electronics. This resulted in spatial undersampling—the vast majority of neurons existed in the dark spaces between electrodes with their activity unobserved.

Finally, a critical inflection point emerged through the integration of MEAs with CMOS technology, which accelerated significantly during the 2000s and reached commercial maturity in the 2010s. By integrating amplification and signal processing circuitry directly onto the silicon substrate, this yielded active MEAs that could both listen to and communicate with neural networks.

2006 · Nobel Prize 2012

Biological Innovation: Induced Pluripotent Stem Cells

In 2006, the laboratory of Shinya Yamanaka identified four specific transcription factors that when introduced into fully differentiated adult cells, could revert them to an embryonic-like pluripotent state.

Click to expand

Yamanaka termed these resulting cells as induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs). This discovery overturned the long-held dogma that cellular differentiation was a terminal, unidirectional process and made a paradigm shift in biology. In 2012, Yamanaka and Sir John B. Gurdon were jointly awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine.

In neuroscience, iPSC technology provided a key to unlocking the black box of the living human brain, giving rise to the powerful "disease in a dish" concept. Scientists could take a simple skin or blood sample from a patient with a neurological disorder, reprogram its cells into iPSCs, and then guide their differentiation into the precise types of neural cells affected by the disease.

On a fundamental level, iPSC technology solves a critical logistical problem for Cortical Labs: sourcing a large and consistent supply of living human cortical neurons, approximately 800,000 per dish. As there is no other ethical or practical method to obtain such quantities of functional human brain cells, the project would have been severely constrained without the scalability provided by iPSCs.

A Theoretical Breakthrough: The Free Energy Principle

The FEP posits that any self-organizing system that manages to persist through time must act in a way that minimizes surprise. This traces an intellectual lineage from Schrödinger through Shannon to Helmholtz.

Click to expand

Food decomposes. Metal oxidizes. Threads fray and batteries discharge. Amid this universal progression toward maximum entropy, how do organisms survive and see these cycles repeat? How do they appear to defy the second law of thermodynamics? This was the paradox Schrödinger first posed in 1944. His answer was that organisms feed upon negative entropy—maintaining internal order by taking in ordered forms like food and energy and exporting disorder such as heat and waste.

While a powerful insight, his formulation was ultimately descriptive rather than explanatory. How does an organism distinguish order from disorder? How does it orchestrate its actions to seek out negentropy? Understanding that process requires a shift from physics to information theory.

The intellectual crucible for information theory was the Second World War. In 1948, Claude Shannon introduced a mathematical measure of information based on entropy. Due to the logarithmic structure of his formula, rare and unpredictable events carry more information than common predictable ones. Later researchers interpreted this as a measure of statistical surprise.

The connection between Shannon's information theory and Schrödinger's negentropy became explicit through physicist Léon Brillouin, who showed that acquiring information about a physical system requires a minimum thermodynamic cost. This reveals that entropy fundamentally measures uncertainty, disorder, and missing information across both physical and informational domains. Now, the problem of "feeding on negentropy" is reframed as a problem of minimizing statistical surprise.

Consider this: if an optician attempted to sell you an optical instrument that projects everything upside-down and backwards, includes a built-in blind spot, and becomes cloudier with age—would you buy it? Yet this defective instrument is the human eye. Hermann von Helmholtz, a 19th century physician, arrived at the same conclusion. His answer was that the eye's failures force the brain to become an active interpreter rather than a passive receiver. The brain makes its best guess about causes of sensory signals through unconscious inference.

The Bayesian Brain Hypothesis emerged as a mathematical framework. The brain employs an internal generative model constantly generating top-down predictions about probable causes of sensory input. These predictions cascade down the cortical hierarchy for comparison with incoming sensory evidence. Mismatches trigger prediction errors that propagate back up.

The Free Energy Principle (FEP), developed by neuroscientist Karl Friston, posits that any self-organizing system that persists must minimize surprise. But organisms cannot compute surprise directly—that would require knowing the true distribution of hidden world states. Instead, the FEP proposes that organisms minimize variational free energy, a more accessible proxy for surprise.

Crucially, minimizing free energy is not just about passively updating beliefs to reduce prediction error (perception); it is equally about actively acting on the world to make it less surprising (active inference). This self-evidencing behaviour is the mechanism for resisting entropy. The FEP provides the "how" for Schrödinger's "what" and brings the story full circle.

Now we see how DishBrain, without evolutionary pressure, goal representation, or prior experience, still learned to play Pong.

04

Significance & Controversies

✦ Why Does It Even Matter?

The true genius of the work lies in how it elegantly solved a fundamental problem in neuroscience. A significant technical hurdle in training in-vitro neurons is the difficulty of providing a conventional reward signal. While one could propose giving the cells a rewarding shot of dopamine for a correct action, achieving this with the precise timing required for effective learning is exceptionally difficult in a petri dish.

Cortical Lab's team leveraged the FEP as the teaching mechanism itself by manipulating the predictability and information entropy of the neurons' sensory environment.

When neurons successfully directed the paddle to hit the ball, the system provided a brief, structured, and therefore predictable (low surprise) electrical stimulus; conversely, missed hits triggered a four-second burst of chaotic white-noise across the array.

Learning is reconceptualized as the system gathering evidence for its generative model, thereby improving its predictive accuracy. In short: self-preservation through self-evidencing.

⚡ What Are The Controversies?

The FEP emerges from a diverse intellectual lineage, positioning itself as a unifying theoretical framework. This breadth leads critics to argue that the FEP functions as an unfalsifiable tautology.

Friston responds by asserting that the FEP is not directly falsifiable in itself but serves as a formal framework that generates specific, falsifiable hypotheses. Critics argue that since a generative model is the set of beliefs, predictions, and priors a system holds and is not directly observable, a proponent of the FEP can always argue that the behavior wasn't surprising from the perspective of the organism's own generative model. This creates an unfalsifiable loop.

Ethical debates have been directed at both the DishBrain and the FEP. The authors used "sentience" for the system because it can sense its environment and act to maintain an internal, predictive model. Critics argue that sentience is the existence of subjective, first-person experience—the authors define sentience by what a system does while critics define it by what a system experiences.

Benefits & Risks

✦ Benefits of SBI

Adaptability & Self-Organization

Neurons grow, prune, and rewire (differentiation, synaptogenesis, apoptosis), letting the network reshape itself to meet functional demands and improve performance as conditions change.

Learning Efficiency & Capacity

Neurons can learn from limited data through spike-timing-dependent plasticity, neuromodulation for context-sensitive learning, and structural plasticity involving forming and removing synapses.

Energy Efficiency

The human brain performs ~1018 operations per second on 20 watts. Achieving the same on the Frontier supercomputer requires 20-40 megawatts and 680 square meters of floor space.

Robustness & Fault Tolerance

Biological systems demonstrate remarkable resilience, often maintaining or regaining functionality even after significant network damage.

⚠ Risks of SBI

Instability & Reproducibility

Even with identical protocols, synthetic biological neural systems form unique connections and show individual responses that change over time as neurons develop or mature.

Integration & Interface Difficulties

Neurons use electrical and chemical signals at millisecond speeds; silicon operates at nanosecond speeds. Scaling up SBI demands advanced bioelectronic interfaces.

Environmental & Biological Support

Cells require tightly controlled nutrients, oxygenation, temperature, and pH, plus sterile operation. Small deviations can stress or kill the culture.

Moral Status & Consciousness

While there is no proof neurons felt pain or pleasure during DishBrain, unpredictable stimuli might be aversive. If future SBI systems show signs of consciousness, we must be cautious.

Commercial Outlook

Near-term · 1–3 Years
Platform Building & First Customers

The primary source of revenue will be research platforms like Cortical Labs' Cortical Cloud and FinalSpark's Neuroplatform. These platforms serve a crucial function by allowing the broader scientific community to validate the technology, discover new applications, and build a talent pipeline—effectively crowdsourcing R&D for the field. Koniku's partnership with Airbus is expected to transition from testing to initial deployments in airports. BBB will focus on demonstrating clear ROI with its F-500 partners.

Mid-term · 3–7 Years
Regulated Markets & Defense Integration

Companies will pivot toward more regulated markets. The most logical path is non-invasive diagnostics. In the defense sector, the continual learning and complex decision-making capabilities of SBI will likely be integrated into operational autonomous systems—drones and robotic vehicles adapting to novel threats without catastrophic forgetting. This period is expected to feature the first wave of M&A, as large tech, defense, and pharmaceutical companies witness the technology's validation.

Long-term · 7+ Years
Foundational Layer of Computing

SBI has the potential to become a foundational layer of the computing and healthcare industries. The energy efficiency could make it the dominant paradigm for AI at the edge, enabling powerful on-device AI in consumer electronics and industrial sensors. The convergence of 3D organoids, advanced brain-computer interfaces, and machine learning could lead to true Organoid Intelligence systems capable of complex problem-solving.

Author's Perspective

"

The development of increasingly powerful, general-purpose AI is now an irreversible industrial and geopolitical reality while the brute-force approach of building ever-larger data centers in response is only a temporary solution that addresses the symptoms, not the underlying architectural disease.

This is why I see the future of advanced intelligence in hybrid architecture. Biological co-processors will provide what silicon cannot and I think this symbiotic relationship offers the only plausible path to solving the AI safety and control problem.

The documented inadequacy of current risk management frameworks is not a temporary flaw, but a systemic failure rooted in the paradox of using software to police itself. A biological co-processor can serve as a physically distinct governor on the behavior of digital superintelligence.

The next, and most critical, phase of today's technological revolution will be the integration of biology.
